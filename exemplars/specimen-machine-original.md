## Why a minus times a minus must be a plus

Nobody believes this rule the first time. You were probably handed it the way
most of us were — "negative times negative is positive, just remember it" — and
something in you filed a quiet objection that was never heard. Good. That
objection is the right place to start, because the mathematicians had it too:
for centuries after negative numbers appeared in Europe, serious scholars called
them "absurd numbers" and avoided them where they could. So if the rule feels
arbitrary to you, you are not behind — you are standing exactly where the
discoverers stood. Let's feel the wall they felt.

**The wall.** Multiplication, as you first learned it, is repeated addition:
3 × 4 means three fours, 4 + 4 + 4 = 12. Fine. Then 3 × (−4) still almost makes
sense: three debts of 4, a debt of 12, so −12. But (−3) × (−4)? *Negative three
copies of a debt?* The picture collapses. You cannot lay out minus-three of
anything on a table. Sit with that for a moment, because this is the honest
situation: the old meaning of multiplication genuinely does not cover this case.
Anyone who tells you it's "obvious" is skipping the actual problem.

**So what do we really get to do?** When a meaning runs out, mathematics does not
discover the answer engraved somewhere — it *chooses* the answer. But it is not a
free choice: the new case must obey the laws that the old cases already obey,
or arithmetic splits into two systems that contradict each other. That is the
whole game, and you can now play it yourself.

**You already trust this law.** Check it with innocent numbers first:

    3 × (5 + 2) = 3 × 7 = 21
    3 × 5 + 3 × 2 = 15 + 6 = 21

Same answer both ways. Multiplying by a sum, or multiplying each piece and adding —
no difference. This is called the distributive law, but forget the name; what
matters is that you just *verified* it, and every computation you have ever done
silently relies on it.

**Now the trap is set.** Compute this one quantity, two ways:

    (−3) × (5 + (−5))

First way: the parenthesis first. 5 + (−5) = 0, and anything times zero is zero.

    (−3) × 0 = 0

Second way: distribute, the same move you just verified:

    (−3) × 5 + (−3) × (−5)

The first piece we already understand — three debts of 5 — so it is −15:

    −15 + (−3) × (−5) = 0

Now look at what the equation is doing to you. Whatever (−3) × (−5) is, it must
be the thing that cancels −15. There is exactly one such number:

    (−3) × (−5) = +15

You were not told the rule. You were cornered into it. The only alternative was
to abandon the distributive law — to accept that (−3) × (5 − 5) gives one answer
computed one way and a different answer computed the other way — and *that* is
the real content of "minus times minus is plus": it is the price of consistency,
not a fact about debts.

**Try to escape, once.** Suppose, out of stubbornness, we decreed
(−3) × (−5) = −15 instead. Run the trap again: the second way now totals
−15 + (−15) = −30, while the first way still says 0. Arithmetic would give two
answers to one question, and you could "prove" anything from the wreckage. The
abandoned road is short, and it ends in a wall; that is *why* it was abandoned.

**Where you now stand.** Notice what kind of thing you just learned, because the
pattern returns at every level of mathematics, all the way up: when a familiar
operation is pushed past the territory where its original picture works, its
extension is chosen — forced, really — by the laws we refuse to give up. The
plus sign at the end of (−3) × (−5) = +15 is not a property of negative numbers.
It is the sound of arithmetic refusing to contradict itself. Keep that sentence;
we will harvest this seed again, the next time a definition seems to come from
nowhere.
