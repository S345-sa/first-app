# Anatomies — what each recommended passage actually does

Original analyses, written by the machine from its understanding of these works'
*methods*. No excerpts here — exact text lives in the learner-supplied files.
The Critic quotes these anatomies against drafts that fall short.

---

## Petzold, *Code* — the flashlight opening
The book's first "computer" is two children who want to talk after bedtime, across
the street, with flashlights. Note what this move does: (1) the reader's existing
knowledge — friendship, windows, blinking lights — is the entire prerequisite list;
(2) the hard problem (encoding) is felt as a *social* problem (how do we say more
things than we have signals?) before it is named; (3) every later abstraction —
codes, bits, relays, logic gates — is a patch the *reader wants* applied to this
one story. The chapter never says "binary representation"; it makes the reader
half-invent it. The thread planted on page one is still load-bearing 300 pages
later when the same lamps become a CPU's bus. **Steal the move, not the lamps:**
open with a need a child could feel, and never let the thread drop.

## Feynman, *Lectures* I — "atoms in motion"
Feynman opens the entire edifice of physics with one sentence-sized bet: if all
knowledge were destroyed and one sentence could be saved, save the atomic
hypothesis. Then — and this is the anatomy — he *cashes* the abstraction
immediately: a drop of water magnified again and again, described at each scale,
until the reader is watching jiggling spheres. Evaporation, dissolving, burning are
then re-told as stories about those spheres. Two methods to steal: (1) every
abstraction is paid for at once in pictures the reader can run as a mental movie;
(2) honesty operates at full power — approximations are flagged as approximations
*at the moment of use*, and what physics does not know is said plainly, with relish
rather than embarrassment.

## *SICP* §1.1 — programs from three primitives
The opening refuses to tour the language. It hands the reader three powers —
primitive expressions, means of combination, means of abstraction — and then
*builds everything in front of them* from those three, naming each construct only
after the reader has already used it. The deep move: the chapter's subject is
secretly epistemology (how complex ideas are built from simple ones), and the
programming language is just the laboratory where the reader verifies every claim
by typing it. **Steal:** give the learner a tiny set of powers, then make every
new thing visibly a combination of powers they already hold (Article 2 in its
purest form).

## *Nand2Tetris* ch. 1 — one gate, then everything
The authors begin by telling the reader, in effect: here is a single trivial
device (NAND); by the end you will have built a computer that plays Tetris, and
you will have built *every layer yourself*. The anatomy: (1) the entire journey is
visible from the first page — motivation by visible summit; (2) nothing is ever
received, only constructed: each chapter's chips are built from the previous
chapter's chips, so Article 1's dependency graph is physically enforced by the
build; (3) specifications are separated from implementations, teaching the reader
that *what* and *how* are different kinds of knowledge.

## *GEB* — the Achilles–Tortoise dialogues
Before every technical chapter, Hofstadter stages a playful dialogue in which the
characters *enact* the coming idea — recursion, self-reference, levels — without
naming it. The reader meets the structure as an experience, then meets it again as
a theory, and the click between the two *is* the understanding. **Steal:** the
two-pass structure (feel it, then formalize it) and the permission it gives an
author to be playful without being imprecise.

## Apostol, *Calculus* I — area before the integral
Apostol commits a famous heresy: integration before differentiation, and before
*that*, a full chapter where the reader computes areas the way Archimedes did —
by trapping a region between inner and outer staircases of rectangles and
squeezing. By the time the integral sign appears, it is a *name for something the
reader has personally done four times*. **Steal:** the definition arrives last,
as a souvenir of work completed, never first as a thing to be unpacked.

## The sharh tradition — the Article-5 gold standards

## Ibn ʿUthaymīn on al-Ājurrūmiyya — 25 pages become 430, and get *easier*
The method: take the matn a clause at a time; restate the clause in plain speech;
give multiple worked examples *with full iʿrāb* so the rule is exercised, not
displayed; raise the objections a student would raise ("then why do we say…?") and
answer them; flag where scholars differ and which view he takes and why. The shock
the learner should sit with: this 17× expansion is the *beginner's* edition. Depth
did not cost accessibility; depth is what *bought* accessibility, because every
gap a beginner would fall into has been pre-filled. This is the direct refutation
of "keep it short for the beginners."

## al-Shāṭibī on the Alfiyya — ten volumes for forty pages
A single line of verse receives: its grammar, its examples, the rival analyses,
the evidence each school musters, the poet's choice and what it commits him to,
and the places the line's wording is itself debatable. The method to steal is the
*completeness contract*: nothing in the source text is allowed to pass unweighed.

## Javadi Amoli, *Tasnim* — 604 pages become ~80 volumes
Per verse: language, then immediate context, then intra-textual connections
(where else the theme appears and how the occurrences interpret each other), then
the layered readings — outward, theological, philosophical, mystical — each
labeled as the layer it is, then the history of interpretation including readings
he rejects *with the reason for rejection stated fairly*. **Steal:** the layers
are explicit and labeled, so the reader always knows which depth they are standing
on (the anatomy of "the reason behind the reason").

## Ibn Rushd's Great Commentaries — the 300–500% density
The structure: the master text in full, word by word — then commentary that is
*larger than the text*: what the words mean, what they must not be taken to mean,
where the Greek and the translation mislead, where al-Fārābī and Ibn Sīnā read it
wrongly and the conceptual root of their error. Two methods to steal: (1) the
commentary corrects *named predecessors with diagnosed errors* — error analysis
as first-class content, exactly what `error-log.md` does for this book's one
reader; (2) the same author also wrote the jāmiʿ (30%) and talkhīṣ (100%)
densities — proof that density is a *choice per audience and purpose*, and that
the masterwork density, the one that earned the title "the Commentator," is the
largest one.
